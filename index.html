Book: Born A Crime by

Trevor Noah

Published on 15 November 2016.

Image Sourced from

amazon

If I could give Born a Crime a new title, it would beat“ Identity During Apartheid”. The word “Identity” describes finding out who you are. ”Apartheid was a system of racial segregation enforced by the white minority government in South Africa from 1948 to 1994. The reason that “Identity During Apartheid” would be the title of the book is because of apartheid it was difficult for Trevor to have an answer to “who am I?” He was born to a white father, Robert, and his mother, Patricia, which didn’t fit into the government's laws. The book shows his struggle with this. The word “Apartheid” puts the story in a specific time and place, so readers can easily know what kind of challenges Trevor faced.

Trevor Noah's description of his step-father: "Abel wanted a traditional marriage with a traditional wife… but he never fell in love with subservient women… 'He only wants a woman who is free because his dream is to put her in a cage.'”, shows that Abel wants a traditional wife but only falling for independent women with the goal of controlling, and taking away their freedom. Even now, there are people who think similar to Abel. They believe that men should be the strong providers and women should focus on home and family.

Like Abel, some people fall in love with someone who is a smart, independent, and successful woman. However, that excitement is confused with a desire to control. They might try to limit their partner's freedom, get jealous of their success, or expect them to be more traditional and dependent women. People who have this mindset say they want equal partnership but still expect their partner to do most of the housework. It shows a contradiction: wanting freedom but wanting a cage.

In some cases, there are women that believe in differentiating roles based on gender. There are mothers that raise their children as they fit with that idea, which makes the daughters later be okay with not having freedom as a woman. Then the idea can be passed from generation to generation, which could lead the whole community to adapt to the perception of separating roles based on gender.

In my opinion, true love and partnership should be about celebrating each other’s freedom. What I have learned from “Born a Crime” is that women can be more successful than men, because Trevor's mother, Patricia, achieved things that Abel couldn’t achieve. So being successful doesn’t depend on someone's gender, but it depends on someone's hard.
